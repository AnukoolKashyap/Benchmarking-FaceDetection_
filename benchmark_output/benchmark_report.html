key takeaways

Queue wait is the bottleneck, not inference. Arch A's mean queue wait of 680ms accounts for 90% of its total latency. The bounded deque was near-permanently full (avg depth 8/10) — frames were being evicted before inference ever touched them.
Parallel workers eliminate the backlog. Arch B's mean queue wait drops to 42ms (p50 = 3ms), meaning workers drain frames almost as fast as they arrive. The queue depth average of 1.3 confirms it was nearly always empty.
Inference itself is faster in B (45ms vs 67ms) despite using the same model. When Arch A's single consumer was fighting queue backlog and OS scheduling pressure, individual predict() calls ran slower. Workers in Arch B had clean execution windows.
Arch B's throughput of 13.85 fps is above the 5 fps target because workers have no artificial throttle. For production, add a rate limiter on the feeder side to cap output at the required rate while keeping the zero-drop property.
Arch B's p99 total latency (409ms) has a tail spike likely from OS thread scheduling jitter on Windows. In a Linux deployment (or with process-level workers) this tail would be significantly reduced.
